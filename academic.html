About Me
About Me

I am an Environmental Engineer holding an M.Sc. in Environmental Engineering awarded jointly by TU Delft, Politecnico di Milano, and FHNW (Switzerland) under the Erasmus programme — fully funded by the European Commission. My research and professional work sit at the interface of fundamental process engineering and applied pilot-scale research in water and wastewater treatment, with a consistent focus on sustainable technologies that recover value from waste streams.

My M.Sc. thesis at TU Delft — supervised by Prof. dr. ir. Jules van Lier and H.L.F.M. Spanjers within the NoChemNar project (EU Horizon 2020 N2kWH continuation) — developed and optimised a pilot-scale bipolar membrane electrodialysis (BPMED) system for industrial wastewater treatment and resource recovery, combining experimental design, mass balance modelling, and pilot-plant commissioning. I subsequently joined AtkinsRéalis (Toronto) as a Process Engineer, where I optimised anaerobic digestion systems at municipal WWTPs — achieving CAD 45,000/year in operational savings and a 5% biogas yield increase — and authored regulatory and design documentation for full-scale wastewater infrastructure projects.

My research focus is on membrane and electrochemical separations, resource recovery, anaerobic and biological treatment, and process modelling and scale-up — the interface between fundamental mechanism and engineered system, where scientific rigour meets real-world translational impact. I bring an uncommon combination of fundamental research training, pilot-plant experimental skills, and full-scale industrial engineering experience, and I am ready to contribute to a world-class research group from day one.

TU Delft — Department of Water Management, NetherlandsNov 2021 – Aug 2023
Research Engineer — NoChemNar Project (EU Horizon 2020 continuation)
Supervisors: Prof. dr. ir. J.B. (Jules) van Lier & H.L.F.M. (Henri) Spanjers
Research context. The NoChemNar project — a continuation of the EU Horizon 2020 N2kWH consortium — addresses a pressing challenge in industrial wastewater treatment: chemical-intensive neutralisation processes generate secondary waste streams and incur high operational costs. My thesis investigated bipolar membrane electrodialysis (BPMED) as an electrochemical alternative that simultaneously treats wastewater and recovers the constituent acids and bases as reusable resources, closing the chemical loop in line with circular economy principles.
  • System design & commissioning: Designed, constructed, and commissioned a laboratory-to-pilot scale BPMED stack including membrane configuration (anion, cation, and bipolar layers), electrode selection, hydraulic flow architecture, and data acquisition — one of few working BPMED pilot systems for industrial streams at TU Delft.
  • Experimental methodology: Applied design of experiments (DoE) to systematically map the operating window across three key parameters — current density, recirculation flow rate, and feed composition — achieving a 12% improvement in recovery efficiency over baseline conditions.
  • Mathematical modelling: Developed a mass balance model describing ionic transport across bipolar membranes under varying feed chemistries. Model validation against experimental data showed <8% deviation, enabling predictive scale-up analysis.
  • Analytical chemistry: Performed ion chromatography (IC) analysis for feed, product, and waste streams; processed data in Python (NumPy, Pandas, Matplotlib) for statistical analysis, concentration profiling, and visualisation.
  • Scientific communication: Authored regulatory-compliant technical reports documenting experimental procedures, results, uncertainty analysis, and scale-up feasibility for EU consortium stakeholders and project milestones.
  • Collaboration: Worked across academic supervisors, industry partners, and the broader N2kWH research consortium, aligning outcomes with consortium deliverables.
